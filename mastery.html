Beyond job-ready

The Path to Mastery

The course you just worked through makes you job-ready. This page is about a different goal — becoming one of the best in the field. That is not more tutorials. It is a handful of hard, real things done in the right order, and a way of working that compounds. Here is the map, and I mean it personally.

The honest part first: nobody reads their way to great. You will get there by shipping things that are a little beyond you, over and over, and letting the struggle teach you. Everything below is built around that one fact.

01 · Your unfair advantage

You are not starting from zero. You are starting from an edge most people in this field will never have.

Most people arriving in GenAI come from research, from web development, or from playing with prompts. What they consistently lack is the thing you have spent ten years doing — and it happens to be exactly what production GenAI is made of. A model demo is easy. A GenAI system that works, at scale, reliably, for real money, is a data and systems problem. That is your home.

Data quality & lineage

Most RAG systems fail at retrieval, not generation — bad chunks, stale data, no lineage. You already think in data quality. That instinct is rare here and worth a fortune.

Pipelines & orchestration

An LLM app is a pipeline: ingest → chunk → embed → index → retrieve → generate → evaluate. You have built and operated pipelines for a decade. You are not learning the shape — you know it.

Scale & cost

You know what breaks at millions of rows and what a query costs. GenAI cost and latency are scale problems wearing a new hat. Most newcomers have never felt scale; you have lived in it.

Reliability & observability

SLAs, monitoring, incident response, on-call. LLMOps is your discipline applied to a new failure mode — a probabilistic one. You bring the operational maturity the field is desperate for.

The move

Do not try to become an ML researcher — that is someone else's edge, and a ten-year detour. Become the person who makes GenAI actually work in production, at scale, reliably. It is the rarest and most valuable profile in the field, and you are already most of the way to it. Everything below sharpens that specific edge.

03 · The practice that separates great from competent

Knowledge is table stakes. Judgment is the moat — and judgment is only built one way: deliberately.

Competent engineers know how things work. The best ones know which thing to reach for, when it will break, and why — and they are usually right. That is judgment, and you cannot read it into existence. You build it with three small weekly habits. None takes long. All of them compound.

Predict, then measure

Before you run any eval, benchmark, or experiment — write down what you expect to happen. Then run it. When reality disagrees with your prediction, you have found the exact edge of your understanding. Chase that gap. This one habit builds calibrated intuition faster than anything else in the field.

Read one system, critique it

Each week, read one real writeup of a production GenAI system — an engineering blog, a post-mortem, a paper's system section. Write three sentences: what would I do differently, and why? You are training the design taste that interviewers and teammates can feel in thirty seconds.

Teach one thing

Explain one thing you learned this week to another person — a colleague, a blog post, a reply to a stranger's question. Teaching is a merciless test: the moment you try to explain it, every gap in your understanding lights up. Close the gaps. This is also how you become known, which is rung five.

04 · Staying at the frontier without drowning

The field moves fast, and the anxiety is real. Here is how the people who last actually handle it.

You do not need to read every paper or try every model. The engineers who stay sharp for years use a two-layer mental model, and they protect the bottom layer with their lives.

Bottom layer — invest here

The durable fundamentals

Tokens, embeddings, attention, sampling, RAG, agents, evaluation, safety, cost. These barely change. A new model still predicts next tokens, still hallucinates, still needs grounding and evals. This is the whole course, deliberately — because it is the part that keeps paying off. Go deep here. Depth in fundamentals is what lets you learn any new thing in an afternoon.

Top layer — treat as lookups

The churning specifics

Which model is best this month, current prices, exact context windows, the newest API. This changes constantly — so do not memorise it. Know it exists, know how to evaluate it against your own use case, and look it up when you need it. Follow five signal sources, not fifty. One 30-minute scan a week is plenty.

The reflex to build: when a shiny new model or technique appears, do not panic and do not chase. Ask the questions you already know how to answer — what capability does this add? what does it cost in money and latency? how would I measure whether it is actually better on my use case? does it change my architecture, or just swap a component? "I'll measure it on my data" beats "I'll adopt it because it's new" every single time. That calm, evaluation-first reflex is what makes you trustworthy to a team — and it is worth more than knowing every headline.

05 · How to know it's working

Honest checkpoints. Not vibes — things that are either true or not.

~3 months

You have shipped a production-grade GenAI system, public, with an eval set and real monitoring. You can hand someone a link and prove — with numbers — that it works.

~6 months

You are the person your team asks about RAG, evals, or LLMOps. You have gone genuinely deep on one area — deep enough to teach it and defend every decision under questioning.

~12 months

You are known outside your own team for depth in something. You can assess a new frontier model calmly and say precisely whether it changes your architecture. You mentor others — which is when you find out how much you actually know.

The quiet tell that you are on the path: you increasingly trust your own judgment over hot takes, and you can always prove your systems work. The day "I think it's better" becomes "here's the eval showing it's better" is the day you stopped being a beginner.
