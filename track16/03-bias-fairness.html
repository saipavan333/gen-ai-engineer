Responsible AI: bias & fairness

Models learn from human data, so they inherit human biases. If your system touches decisions about people — hiring, lending, content, access — that isn’t an abstract ethics topic; it’s an engineering requirement you’ll be asked about. This lesson is practical: where bias comes from, how to measure it, and how to reduce it.

Why models are biased

An LLM learns statistical patterns from vast amounts of human-written text, and that text reflects the world’s inequities, stereotypes, and skews. So the model can absorb and reproduce them: associating certain jobs with certain genders, rating dialects as less “professional,” performing worse for some groups because they’re underrepresented in the data. This isn’t the model being malicious — it’s a mirror of its training data, which is exactly why it’s subtle and pervasive. The harm becomes real when such a model is placed in a decision: screening résumés, moderating speech, approving applications, or simply representing people. As the builder, you’re responsible for the system’s behavior, not just the model’s.

You can’t manage what you don’t measure

Fairness starts as an evaluation problem — Track 6, pointed at equity. The core technique is disaggregated evaluation: don’t just measure overall accuracy, measure it per group. A résumé-screener that’s 90% accurate overall might be 95% for one group and 78% for another — a serious problem the aggregate number hides completely. So you build test sets that let you compare performance across the relevant dimensions (and test with matched inputs that differ only in a sensitive attribute — same résumé, different name — to see if the output changes when it shouldn’t). You measure, break it down, and look specifically for gaps.

disaggregated_eval.py
# same qualifications, names signalling different groups — output should not differ
for group, names in test_names.items():
    scores = [screen(resume.replace("NAME", n)) for n in names]
    print(group, "avg score:", mean(scores))
# a large gap across groups on identical qualifications = bias to fix

Reducing it (defense in depth, again)

No single fix makes a system “fair”. You layer mitigations and keep measuring.

  • Data — for anything you fine-tune, curate for balance and representation.
  • Prompting — instruct the model to ignore irrelevant attributes and judge on stated qualifications.
  • Output checks — validate and monitor decisions for disparate patterns. This is Track 7’s output validation, aimed at fairness.
  • Human oversight — keep a person in the loop for consequential decisions rather than fully automating them.

Transparency: document what the system does, its known limitations, and how it was tested — a practice often formalized as a model card. And crucially, question whether an LLM should make the decision at all — sometimes the responsible design is to assist a human, not replace their judgment.

Key points
  • Models inherit bias from human training data — it’s a mirror, which makes it subtle and pervasive.
  • Harm becomes real when the system makes or influences decisions about people; you own the system’s behavior.
  • Measure with disaggregated evaluation (per group), and test matched inputs differing only in a sensitive attribute.
  • Reduce it in layers: data, prompting, output checks, human oversight, transparency (model cards).
  • Sometimes the responsible choice is to assist a human, not automate the decision.
Practice

Your team ships an AI résumé-screener and reports “90% accuracy — great!” What single evaluation would you insist on before it touches real candidates, and what would you do if it revealed a gap?

▸ Show solution & reasoning

The evaluation: disaggregated accuracy — break the 90% down by the groups that matter (gender, ethnicity signals, age proxies, etc.), and run matched tests where identical qualifications carry only a different name or school. The aggregate number is meaningless for fairness because it can hide a large per-group gap: 90% overall could be 96% for one group and 75% for another.

If it reveals a gap: do not ship as-is. Investigate the cause (biased training/prompt, a proxy feature like zip code or name doing the discriminating), apply mitigations (remove/neutralize the proxy, adjust the prompt to focus on qualifications, add output checks), and re-measure. Add human review so no candidate is auto-rejected on the model’s word alone, and document the findings and limits in a model card.

If the gap can’t be reliably closed, the responsible decision may be to not automate screening and instead have the tool assist a human reviewer. The through-line: for decisions about people, “accurate on average” is not the bar — “equitable across groups, verified, and overseen” is, and proving that is an engineering task, not a slogan.

Why is a single overall-accuracy number insufficient for judging whether a people-affecting AI system is fair?

  • A) A high average can hide large disparities between groups; you need accuracy disaggregated per group to see them.
    Correct. Overall accuracy averages across everyone, so a system that works well for one group and poorly for another can post a great aggregate while treating some people unfairly. Only per-group (disaggregated) measurement reveals the gap — which is the thing that matters for fairness.
  • B) Overall accuracy is always wrong and should never be measured.
    It’s still useful as one signal — the issue is that it’s insufficient for fairness on its own, not that it’s worthless. You add disaggregation, you don’t discard aggregate metrics.
  • C) Because fairness can’t be measured at all.
    Fairness can be measured (imperfectly) — disaggregated evals, matched-input tests, and disparity metrics all quantify it. Claiming it’s unmeasurable is an excuse not to try.
  • D) Because accuracy and fairness are the same thing.
    They’re different: a model can be accurate on average yet unfair across groups. Conflating them is exactly the mistake disaggregated evaluation exists to catch.
Deep dive: fairness is contextual, and it’s a sociotechnical problem

One reason this is hard — and worth being honest about in an interview — is that “fair” has no single mathematical definition that satisfies every intuition. Researchers have formalized several fairness criteria (equal accuracy across groups, equal false-positive rates, equal opportunity, and others), and a famous result is that some of them are mutually incompatible: you often cannot satisfy all reasonable definitions at once, so which one applies depends on the context and the harms involved. That means fairness isn’t a checkbox you compute; it’s a judgment about your specific application — what decision is being made, who bears the cost of each kind of error, and what the domain and the law require.

A thoughtful engineer names the relevant notion of fairness for the use case rather than pretending there’s a universal metric.

The deeper point is that bias is a sociotechnical problem: it lives in the interaction between the model, the data, the product design, and the humans and institutions around it — not in the weights alone. So the strongest mitigations are often not clever algorithms but design decisions: keeping a human in the loop, giving affected people a way to appeal, choosing not to build a high-risk automated decision, being transparent about limitations, and including diverse perspectives when defining what “good” means. This connects straight to the next lesson: because these harms are real and hard, they’re increasingly the subject of regulation, which turns “we should be responsible” into “we’re legally required to be.” Treating responsibility as a first-class engineering requirement — measured, documented, and designed for — is both the ethical stance and, increasingly, the compliant one.
