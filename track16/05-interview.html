Interview check: Frontier & Responsible AI

These questions test something subtle: not just whether you can build, but whether you have judgment — about new capabilities, trade-offs, and the consequences of what you ship. This is often what separates a senior hire from a mid one.

How to use this

Answer aloud first. The bar for this track: can you reason calmly about a fast-moving field and about the human impact of your systems — showing maturity, not hype and not hand-wringing? That balance is exactly what these questions probe.

The questions teams ask

Q1 · Staying current

“What are reasoning models, and when would you use one?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: They’re models trained to produce a long internal chain-of-thought before answering — spending “test-time compute” to think and self-correct, which sharply improves hard math, code, and logic. The trade is cost and latency, since you pay for all that thinking. So I’d route by difficulty: use a reasoning model for genuinely hard, low-volume problems and a cheaper/smaller model for simple, high-volume tasks — and confirm the split with evals, paying for reasoning only where it moves accuracy.

Testing: That you understand the second scaling lever (inference-time compute) and treat it as a cost-aware routing decision, not a default.

Q2 · The hype filter

“Context windows are huge now — is RAG dead?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: No — it shifts the balance, not the principle. Large context helps for bounded, self-contained content, but pasting everything is slow, expensive, distraction-prone (“lost in the middle”), can’t handle changing data, and won’t fit most corpora. Retrieval remains how you give the model just the relevant, current slice cheaply. I’d use big context to simplify small cases and keep RAG for scale and freshness — matching the tool to the data.

Testing: Whether you resist hype and reason from cost, latency, accuracy, and freshness rather than chanting a slogan.

Q3 · A real design choice

“Open-weight model you host vs a closed API — how do you choose?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: Trade-off. Closed APIs often have the highest peak capability and zero infra, but you send data out, pay per token, and can’t control versioning. Open-weight models you run give privacy (data stays in your environment), control, predictable cost at scale, and no lock-in, at the price of running infrastructure and sometimes lower peak capability. So: privacy-sensitive or high-volume internal tools lean open/local; low-volume features needing top capability lean closed. I’d decide per use case and validate on our own evals.

Testing: That you can weigh privacy, cost, control, and capability — not just reach for the biggest API.

Q4 · Responsibility (they’re watching your judgment)

“We’re building an AI to screen job candidates. What are your concerns?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: This is high-risk — it affects livelihoods and can inherit bias from training data. Before shipping I’d run disaggregated evaluation (accuracy per group, plus matched résumés differing only by name/school) to detect disparate treatment, remove proxy features doing hidden discrimination, keep a human in the loop so no one is auto-rejected on the model’s word, document limitations in a model card, and build audit logs. It’s also likely regulated as high-risk, which mandates much of this. If a fairness gap can’t be closed, the responsible choice is to assist reviewers rather than automate the decision.

Testing: Maturity. They want to hear disaggregated evaluation, human oversight, documentation, and the willingness to not fully automate a consequential decision.

Q5 · Regulation literacy

“How does something like the EU AI Act affect how you build?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: It’s risk-based — obligations scale with the use case’s potential harm. So the first thing I do is identify the tier: unacceptable (banned), high-risk (hiring/credit/medical — strict rules), limited (disclose it’s AI), or minimal (free). For high-risk, I build in evaluation, human oversight, documentation, and audit logging from the start, because retrofitting is painful and most of it is just responsible engineering made auditable. I’d also mind data privacy (GDPR) and sector rules. I’m not a lawyer, so for real decisions I’d loop in legal — but I design so compliance is achievable.

Testing: That you know regulation is risk-tiered and that compliance overlaps heavily with evals, oversight, and logging you already do.

Q6 · The meta-question

“The field changes so fast — how do you keep up without burning out?”

▸ Strong answer & what they’re testing

Answer: Two layers. Fundamentals — tokens, embeddings, RAG, agents, evaluation, safety, cost — barely change, so I keep those deep; a new model still predicts tokens, hallucinates, and needs grounding and evals. Specifics — best model, prices, context sizes — churn, so I treat them as lookups and check when needed. When something new appears, I ask principled questions: what capability does it add, what does it cost, and how do I evaluate it on my use case? That “measure it on my data” reflex keeps me current and grounded instead of chasing headlines.

Testing: That you have a durable, evaluation-first mindset — the thing that makes someone reliable over years, not just this quarter.

Rapid-fire self-check

A reasoning model and a small classifier both cost money. When is paying for the reasoning model justified?

  • A) On genuinely hard, multi-step problems where its extra test-time thinking improves accuracy enough to justify the higher cost and latency.
    Correct. Reasoning models earn their cost on hard math/code/logic/planning; for simple, high-volume tasks a small model is faster, cheaper, and just as good. It’s a per-task routing decision validated by evals.
  • B) Always — reasoning models are strictly better, so use them everywhere.
    They’re slower and pricier, and add no benefit on simple tasks. Using them everywhere wastes money and latency — the opposite of good routing.
  • C) Never — thinking longer can’t improve answers.
    It demonstrably does on hard problems; that’s the whole point of test-time compute. The nuance is that it’s not worth it for easy tasks, not that it never helps.
  • D) Only for formatting and classification tasks.
    Those simple tasks are exactly where a reasoning model is wasted. It pays off on complex, multi-step reasoning, not lightweight formatting.

Which is the strongest signal of responsibility when building an AI that makes consequential decisions about people?

  • A) Disaggregated evaluation across groups, human oversight, documentation, and a willingness not to fully automate if fairness can’t be assured.
    Correct. Measuring per-group performance, keeping humans in the loop, documenting limits, and declining to automate when it isn’t safe are the concrete practices that make a people-affecting system responsible — and they overlap with high-risk compliance requirements.
  • B) A high overall accuracy number.
    Aggregate accuracy can hide serious per-group disparities. It’s necessary but nowhere near sufficient for fairness on decisions about people.
  • C) Using the largest available model.
    Model size doesn’t confer fairness or oversight — a bigger model can be just as biased. Responsibility comes from measurement, oversight, and design, not scale.
  • D) Shipping fast and fixing issues if users complain.
    For consequential decisions, “ship and wait for complaints” risks real harm to real people. Responsible practice catches disparities before deployment via disaggregated evaluation and oversight.

A résumé-screening model reports 83% overall accuracy. Why isn’t that number enough to ship it?

  • You must disaggregate by group — a healthy overall accuracy can hide one group being failed badly.
    Correct. Averages built from groups can mask a large per-group disparity on matched inputs; measure and gate on the gap, not just the aggregate.
  • 83% is simply below the 90% bar.
    The point is disaggregation, not the absolute number.
  • Accuracy is the wrong metric entirely.
    Accuracy is fine; you also need it broken down by group.
  • It needs a bigger training set.
    More data doesn’t reveal a hidden disparity — disaggregated evaluation does.

When should you NOT let an LLM automate a decision?

  • When it’s high-risk or irreversible and a known disparity or failure can’t be closed — keep a human in the loop.
    Correct. ‘We shipped knowing it was unfair/unsafe’ is the worst position; assist a reviewer rather than automate the consequential call.
  • Whenever accuracy is below 100%.
    Perfect accuracy isn’t the bar; risk and reversibility are.
  • Never — automate everything for speed.
    Consequential, irreversible decisions warrant human oversight.
  • Only if the model is small.
    Model size doesn’t determine whether automation is appropriate.
