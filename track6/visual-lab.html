Watch an eval gate catch a regression

The scariest bug in an LLM product is the one that passes review. Watch a harmless-looking prompt change sail past the average score — and get caught by a per-case gate that's watching the two things that actually broke.

How to use this lab

Press ▶ Play to run the golden set against two prompt versions, or Step through each stage. Watch the v2 column: the overall pass rate barely moves, but two specific cases flip to fail.

Make it yours

1. The overall score only drops a few points from v1 to v2. Why is that more dangerous than a big drop, not less?
2. One of the two regressions is the out-of-scope refusal. Why does a broken refusal matter more than a broken answer?

▸ Show reasoning

A small average drop is dangerous precisely because it's easy to wave through — 'we're still at 75%, ship it.' But averages are made of individual cases, and a change that fixes tone while quietly breaking the refusal has the same average as one that's genuinely fine. The gate blocks on any previously-passing case that now fails, so it catches the regression the average buried. A broken refusal is worse than a broken answer because it means the system now confidently handles things it was specifically built to decline — that's how an assistant ends up giving advice, or an action, it should have refused.

What you just saw
  • A golden set as the fixed yardstick for every change.
  • An average that looks fine while specific cases regress.
  • A per-case gate blocking the merge, refusal included.
