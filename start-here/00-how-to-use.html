How to use this course

Two minutes of orientation that will make every lesson after this one land harder. Read it once, then never worry about “am I doing this right?” again.

Why this course exists

There has never been a better time to build software that thinks in language. A single person with a laptop can now build a research assistant, a customer-support agent, or a tool that reads a thousand contracts before lunch. The thing standing between “I’ve heard of ChatGPT” and “I shipped a reliable AI product” is not talent or a fancy degree — it’s a clear path and the discipline to walk it.

That’s what this is. A path from zero to a job-ready applied generative-AI engineer — someone who can take a fuzzy idea (“make our docs answerable”) and turn it into something real, fast, and trustworthy. I’ll teach you the way the best mentors teach: motivation first (why does this thing exist, what problem does it kill), then the idea, then proof, then practice. No skipped steps. No vocabulary used before it’s defined.

The one test that matters

After every lesson, ask yourself: “Could I explain this to a friend, and could I use it on the job?” If yes, you’ve got it. If not, the lesson isn’t finished with you yet — re-read the concept, redo the practice. That’s the whole standard. Everything in this course is built to get you to “yes.”

The five habits that make this work

I’ve watched a lot of people learn this material. The ones who become dangerous-in-a-good-way all do the same five things:

  1. Type the code yourself. Copy-paste teaches your clipboard, not your brain. Typing forces you to read every line.
  2. Predict before you run. Before hitting run, say out loud what you expect. When you’re wrong, you’ve found exactly the thing you didn’t understand.
  3. Do the practice before peeking. The struggle is the point. A solution you reached for after trying teaches ten times what a solution you read cold does.
  4. Explain it to someone (or a rubber duck). If you can’t say it simply, you don’t have it yet. This is the “could I explain this?” test in action.
  5. Go in order, and don’t rush. There’s no deadline here. Quality of understanding beats speed of completion, every single time.

You finish a lesson on tokens. You can recite the definition, but you couldn’t explain to a friend why token counts matter for cost. By this course’s standard, what should you do?

  • A) Move on — you got the definition, that’s good enough.
    This is the trap. The course’s test isn’t “can you recite it,” it’s “could you explain it and use it on the job.” Cost is exactly the kind of practical consequence a real engineer is expected to reason about.
  • B) Re-read the concept and redo the example until you could explain the “why” simply.
    Correct. “Could I explain this to a friend?” is the whole bar. A gap in the why means the lesson isn’t finished with you — and that’s completely normal. Loop back; it’s cheap and it works.
  • C) Skip ahead to fine-tuning, which sounds more advanced and impressive.
    Jumping tracks breaks the path. Fine-tuning leans on everything in Tracks 1–2; arriving there with a shaky grasp of tokens just moves the confusion downstream.
  • D) Memorize the token definition word-for-word for the quiz.
    Memorizing words is the weakest form of knowing. The quizzes are designed so that rote memorization won’t save you — understanding will.
